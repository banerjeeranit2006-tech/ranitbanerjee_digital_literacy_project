## Crime Type: UPI / Online Payment Fraud

## Case Study: "The Fake Scholarship Credit" — A Realistic Scenario

### What the Crime Is
UPI payment fraud is a form of financial cybercrime in which criminals deceive victims into either sending money themselves or revealing authentication credentials — such as a UPI PIN or OTP — by impersonating trusted authorities like banks, universities, or government departments. Unlike traditional hacking, this crime exploits human psychology rather than technical weaknesses.

### How It Typically Happens (Step by Step)
1. A first-year student receives a WhatsApp message from an unknown number, claiming to be from their university's "Student Welfare and Scholarship Cell."
2. The message states that a scholarship of Rs. 8,000 has been sanctioned in the student's name and must be "activated" within 24 hours.
3. The fraudster sends a UPI payment *request* — not a payment — framed as a "one-rupee identity verification step."
4. To appear credible, the fraudster also shares a fake university letterhead and a screenshot of a fabricated PFMS (Public Financial Management System) portal.
5. The student, excited and under time pressure, enters their UPI PIN to "confirm" the request.
6. Instead of receiving any scholarship, the student's account is debited. The fraudster repeats the request multiple times within seconds.
7. In more advanced variants, the student is told to share an OTP "to complete the scholarship transfer," which gives the fraudster full access to the linked bank account.

### Who Is Usually Targeted
First-year college students who are unfamiliar with how UPI actually works, rural and semi-urban users, and students who have recently filled out scholarship or financial aid forms online — as their contact details are sometimes exposed through data leaks or unverified portals.

### Consequences
Victims typically lose between Rs. 2,000 and Rs. 50,000 depending on account balance and transaction limits. Beyond the financial loss, victims experience significant emotional distress, embarrassment, and loss of trust in legitimate digital services. Many delay reporting out of shame, which allows the fraudster to target additional victims using the same script.
